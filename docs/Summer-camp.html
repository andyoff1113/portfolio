Evaluating a New Pandemic Influenza Outbreak in Kyoto

A Simulation Exercise — Summer Bootcamp of Infectious Disease Modeling, Kyoto 2024

Andy Chu (Group 2)

2024-08-10

This was a group project from the Summer Bootcamp of Infectious Disease Modeling (Kyoto, 2024): a 10-day intensive course where each group was assigned a different infectious disease to model and analyze, presenting their findings on the final day. Group 2 — Zhen Zhi Chu (Andy), Jongmin Lee, Thein Min Swe, Sachiko Ichihara, Yuri Amemiya, and Keita Shimizu — was assigned a hypothetical new pandemic influenza outbreak reaching Kyoto, and modeled how it would respond to different public health interventions.

Andy’s main contributions were designing the simulation scenarios and writing the ODE formulas. Simulations were run in Berkeley Madonna, and Andy was also mainly responsible for tuning the simulation software and producing the resulting charts.

Introduction

Pandemic influenza differs from seasonal influenza in a key way: with no pre-existing immunity in the population, historical pandemics (Spanish flu 1918–20, Asian flu 1957–58, Hong Kong flu 1968–69, Russian flu 1977–79, and H1N1/09) have caused far larger and less predictable waves than any typical winter season. H1N1/09 itself combined swine, human, and avian gene segments and spread worldwide between April 2009 and August 2010, with roughly 491,000 lab-confirmed cases and 18,000 deaths reported by WHO. In Japan, the first confirmed cases (high school students with no travel history) triggered a rapid escalation to nationwide school closures within days.

The exercise imagined a new, unknown influenza-like illness with epidemiological features similar to 2009 pandemic influenza, already five weeks into an outbreak in Kobe and Osaka, with one infected child returning to Kyoto. The group’s task was to evaluate outbreak preparedness and response options — school closure, mask-wearing, and vaccination — before the disease could take hold in Kyoto.

Methods

The group built a deterministic, age-structured (adult / child) compartmental model stratified further by vaccination status, with ordinary differential equations governing transitions between susceptible, infected, recovered, and dead states. Parameters were grounded in Kyoto’s 2009 census (adult population 2,275,000; child population 347,000) and literature estimates: a basic reproduction number R₀ = 1.482 (age-dependent next-generation matrix), a mean generation time of 4 days, mask-wearing impact from coverage × efficacy (0.5 × 0.3), and one-dose vaccine effectiveness against susceptibility, infectiousness, and death (α₁ = 0.6, α₂ = 0.5, α₃ = 0.18). Six scenarios were simulated over a one-year period: no intervention; school closure alone (3 weeks); mask-wearing alone (all year); and three mask-plus-vaccination combinations differing in mask duration and how early vaccination began.

Results

With no intervention, the epidemic peaked around day 98 with a final size of about 301,000 infections (11.5% of the 2.6 million population) and 11 deaths. School closure alone barely changed the outcome — it delayed the peak by about three weeks but left the final size and death toll essentially unchanged, since the intervention window was too short relative to the epidemic’s overall duration. Mask-wearing alone was far more effective on its own, cutting the final size to about 7.3% and deaths to 6, by delaying and lowering the peak throughout the year.

Combining mask-wearing with vaccination performed best overall, especially when vaccination started earlier: starting mask-wearing from day 0 and vaccination from day 150 (rather than day 180) produced the smallest final size (6.6%) and fewest deaths (5) of all six scenarios — about a 43% reduction in final size and 55% reduction in deaths relative to doing nothing.

Discussion & Recommendations

School closure is useful for containing localized clusters but is difficult to sustain for long periods, limiting its stand-alone impact across a full epidemic wave. Mask-wearing (as a public health and social measure) can meaningfully lower and delay the epidemic peak and ease the burden on hospitals, though even 100% coverage cannot push R_t below 1 on its own. Vaccination directly removes people from the susceptible pool and has both direct and indirect population-level effects, but timing matters — earlier vaccination consistently outperformed later vaccination in the simulations. The group’s overall recommendation was a three-pronged, coordinated approach: timely school closure around the epidemic peak, mask-wearing from day 0 sustained throughout the outbreak, and vaccination rolled out as early as possible — since combined interventions consistently outperformed any single measure alone.

Limitations: the exercise modeled a hypothetical, not a real, pathogen; the main analysis used simplified, constant intervention parameters without sensitivity analysis or confidence intervals; and it did not model human behavior change (e.g., spontaneous self-isolation). An appendix extension explored a stochastic version of the model (Gillespie algorithm) to characterize the probability of outbreak extinction versus sustained epidemic spread under different importation and intervention-timing assumptions.
